A six-module, blended-delivery AI training curriculum designed for immigrant-owned micro and small businesses, grounded in Kolb's Experiential Learning Cycle and Fink's Taxonomy of Significant Learning.

Learning Goals

What learners should become

Derived from Fink's Taxonomy of Significant Learning. These goals move beyond information transmission toward application, integration, self-awareness, and the development of new values — reinforcing one another across the six modules.

Foundational Knowledge & Skills
Understanding and using AI in simple, informed ways
  • Understand the different types of AI — generative AI, retrieval-augmented generation, agentic systems — and how they work in plain terms
  • Use common AI tools at varying levels of sophistication and make informed decisions about which tools to use for different tasks
Integration Goals
Connecting AI to business realities
  • Understand how AI is already shaping their lives, businesses, and industries — and how this may evolve
  • Identify where AI can be meaningfully integrated into their own workflows
Application Goals
Evaluating risk and building strategy
  • Evaluate the risks and implications of AI use — including accuracy, privacy, labour displacement, and bias
  • Develop strategies for incorporating AI in ways that augment, rather than replace, human effort and ingenuity
Caring Goals
Developing responsibility and civic agency
  • Develop a sense of responsibility around AI use and understand their role in shaping how these technologies are developed and governed
Learning How to Learn
Building capacity beyond the program
  • Build the capacity to continue developing their knowledge and practical use of AI independently beyond the duration of this program
Assessment Approach
Educative, not auditive
  • Forward-looking — apply knowledge to realistic future-facing situations; simulate real business scenarios
  • Self-assessment — metacognitive reflection at the close of each module; shared at the start of the next
Participant Personas

Four archetypes. One program.

Derived from survey data (n=672) and grounded in Rogers' Diffusion of Innovations, Van Dijk's Digital Divide theory, BCG's AI adoption stages, and Curry's Onion Model of learning styles. These are not fixed categories — they are flexible guides for facilitators.

Framework Reference
Kolb's Experiential Learning Cycle
Concrete experience — Doing; direct encounter with a task or situation
Reflective observation — Watching; reviewing and reflecting on the experience
Abstract conceptualisation — Thinking; forming general principles from reflection
Active experimentation — Planning; applying concepts to new situations
Fink's Taxonomy of Significant Learning
Foundational knowledge — Understanding and remembering information
Application — Using knowledge, skills, and creative thinking
Integration — Connecting ideas, people, and realms of life
Human dimension — Learning about oneself and how to interact with others
Caring — Developing new feelings, interests, and values
Learning how to learn — Becoming a better, self-directed learner
