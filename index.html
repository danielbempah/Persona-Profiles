Theoretical Foundations

In program design and curriculum development, it is important to recognize that even though similar, program beneficiaries are not a monolith. While they may share surface-level similarities, meaningful differences in experience, capacity, and perception shape how they engage with new tools and ideas. For this project, personas were created based on AI/digital literacy, prior usage, attitudes toward AI and digital tools, preferred learning styles, and organizational characteristics. This segmentation is grounded in four complementary theoretical frameworks.

Framework Key Contribution to Persona Design
Rogers' Diffusion of Innovations Focuses on how the perceived value and impact of new technology, coupled with organizational culture, influences rate of adoption. Used here as a spectrum rather than rigid categories — describing psychological and behavioral readiness to adopt new technologies.
Digital Divide Theory (Jan van Dijk) Provides a layered understanding of technological access, distinguishing between motivational, material, skills-based, and usage barriers. Demonstrates that access to tools alone does not ensure meaningful adoption — a critical lens for this immigrant-owned micro-enterprise population.
BCG AI Adoption Stage Model Maps the path to AI adoption into five stages progressing from traditional workflows (Information Assistance, Task Assistance, Delegation) to AI agentic workflows (Semiautonomous Collaboration, Autonomous Orchestration). Matches five distinct employee personas to each stage, adapted here for small business operators.
Curry's Learning Styles Onion Model Layers instructional preference (preferred choice of learning environment), social interaction preference during learning, information processing style (individual intellectual approach to processing information), and cognitive personality style (core personality and how it influences learning). Informs how potential participants will engage with the program — used as tendencies, not deterministic categories.

Taken together, these frameworks informed the development of four participant personas derived from small business survey data. These personas are neither exhaustive nor mutually exclusive; rather, they serve as flexible guides, acknowledging that individuals may exhibit overlap, hybridity, and variation in practice.

Theoretical Composite

On Rogers' spectrum, this persona sits firmly at the Laggard end — but not because of temperament. The Digital Divide literature (van Dijk) is indispensable here: the barrier is first-order, rooted in access to infrastructure, language, and institutional trust, not in psychological resistance. BCG's Stage 1 (Information Assistance) is the ceiling, not the floor — most in this group have not meaningfully tried AI and cannot yet distinguish it from a generic internet search. Through Curry's Onion Model, instructional preference sits at the outermost layer: they require in-person, socially embedded, language-appropriate environments before any deeper learning can take hold. The challenge is not capability. It is proximity — to the right tool, at the right moment, with the right guide.

Persona POV

Lucy has been running the laundromat for eleven years. The phone never stops. There is always something — a supplier, a customer, a broken machine — pulling her attention in three directions at once. Someone once showed her a translation app and she used it for a week, then forgot. AI, to her, is a word that belongs to a different world: sleek offices, fast people, English speakers. Not hers. Not yet. She is not afraid of it — she just cannot see where the door is.

Key Differentiating Signals
Digital comfort: Struggles with or actively avoids technology
AI tools tried: None, or Google Translate only
Learning mode: In-person, one-on-one, in native language
Primary challenge: Customer acquisition, managing finances, finding time
Language: Non-English preference — Spanish or Chinese
Business sector: Personal services, food/retail — high operational load, low administrative slack

This is the most populous persona in the sample — the quiet middle. On Rogers' spectrum, they sit in the Early/Late Majority zone: aware, not yet committed. BCG places them at Stage 2–3 (Task Assistance to early Delegation) — they have tried a tool, perhaps ChatGPT for a caption or Canva's AI features for a flyer, but the use has been episodic, not embedded. Van Dijk's motivational divide is the operative barrier: they believe AI could help, but they need proof — proof it is safe, proof it saves time, proof it is not going to replace something they have built carefully. Through Curry's model, their information processing style favors concrete, sequential demonstration over abstract concept — the "so what for my business" must be immediate and visible before they will commit sustained attention.

Persona POV

Mustafa knows what ChatGPT is. He has used it once, maybe twice, to draft an email he was not sure how to word. The result was decent. He closed the tab and went back to work. It is not that he distrusts it. It is that trust, for him, is built slowly — through evidence that accumulates gradually, through use. Show him one business like his, in his neighborhood, that uses AI every week and saves four hours. That might be enough.

Key Differentiating Signals
Digital comfort: Comfortable — can figure most things out
AI tools tried: 1–2 tools (ChatGPT, Grammarly, or Canva AI features)
AI uses: Writing/editing text, social media content — isolated, not systematic
Motivators: "Seeing examples from businesses like mine" and "proof it will save me time"
Concerns: Accuracy of outputs, data privacy and security
Learning preference: In-person workshop, one-on-one coaching, working alongside someone

Here, the Rogers curve accelerates. These respondents sit in the Early Adopter zone — they have crossed the threshold from awareness to active experimentation, but are still building fluency. BCG Stage 3 (Delegation) is their current home: they assign defined tasks to AI — email drafts, image generation, research queries — but have not yet restructured their workflow around it. The Digital Divide is no longer the primary barrier; the gap is now one of skills (van Dijk's second-order divide) rather than access. Through Curry's model, their social interaction preference is active and peer-oriented, with a strong information processing tendency toward active experimentation — they learn by doing, by breaking things, by trying a new tool before reading the manual. What they need is not permission. It is scaffolding.

Persona POV

Bridget runs the tutoring business alone — curriculum, marketing, invoicing, everything. She found Claude six months ago and it felt, in her own words, like getting an extra pair of hands. Not a replacement. An assistant. She has started to map out where else it might fit, poking at the edges of her workflow like someone testing ice before stepping onto a frozen pond. She is ready to go further. She just needs to know the ice will hold.

Key Differentiating Signals
Digital comfort: Very comfortable — enjoys using technology
AI tools tried: 3 or more (ChatGPT, Claude, Canva AI, Grammarly, Gemini)
AI uses: Writing, research, social media, customer communications — multi-purpose but not yet systematic
Motivators: "Having someone walk me through it" and free or low-cost tools
Learning preference: Self-paced online videos, virtual workshops, coaching — multimodal
Primary time sink: Content creation and administrative tasks — areas most ripe for AI delegation

Rare in this sample — but present. On Rogers' curve, these are Innovators and Early Adopters who have already crossed into BCG's Stage 4 (Semiautonomous Collaboration): AI is not a tool they reach for occasionally, it is woven into how work gets done. Van Dijk's divide barely registers; their barriers, where they exist, are about quality and reliability of outputs — not access or confidence. Through Curry's Onion Model, their cognitive personality style reflects strong internal processing: they think carefully about what AI is doing, push back when outputs disappoint, and iterate deliberately. They are the peer models BCG identifies as catalysts for organizational adoption. In a small-business context, they are the proof of concept the program can point to.

Persona POV

The translation tools came first — a practical decision, a bridge between languages in a city where commerce runs on code-switching. Then came the content generation, then the automations. Now AI is less a feature of how Lee works and more the ambient condition of it, like electricity — unremarkable until it is gone. She does not evangelize. She just produces, reliably, more than she once thought was possible for one person.

Key Differentiating Signals
Digital comfort: Very comfortable — enjoys using technology
AI tools tried: 4 or more tools across multiple categories
AI uses: Full stack — writing, research, image generation, customer communications, translation, automation
Concerns (if any): Cost of AI tools and output accuracy at advanced use cases — not adoption itself
Learning preference: Self-directed; interested in automation, workflow integration, and advanced use
Business sector: Education, professional services, creative industries — knowledge-intensive sectors
Synthesis
The Logic Holding This Together

The spine of this framework is the recognition that readiness and access are not the same thing — and that conflating them is how programs design solutions for the wrong problem. A structurally excluded owner and a cautious pragmatist may both show low AI adoption, but one needs a language-accessible entry point and the other needs a compelling use case. The four-persona model honors that distinction. What it reveals — that a demographic breakdown cannot — is that this population is not simply "behind." It is differentiated, and the shape of its differentiation follows the contours of structural inequality as much as individual psychology. The intervention logic flows directly from this: different personas require different doors, not a bigger megaphone at the same door.
